# Spencer Pratt Is Not the Story

## The reality-TV mayoral candidate's most-watched ads weren't made by his campaign. That's the story — and 2028 is going to be built on it.

---

The most-watched political ad in the 2026 Los Angeles mayoral race is a ninety-second video that casts the city as Christopher Nolan's Gotham. Mayor Karen Bass wears Joker makeup. Governor Gavin Newsom eats cake like Marie Antoinette. Kamala Harris chugs vodka. Citizens are dragged before this elite, plead for help with homelessness and wildfire recovery, get ridiculed for their trouble, and are then rescued by a caped figure who turns out to be Spencer Pratt — yes, that Spencer Pratt, of *The Hills* — leading a tomato-throwing crowd to take back the city.

By the time you read this, the ad has somewhere north of 3.9 million views on X. Jeb Bush called it "maybe the best political ad of the year." Ted Cruz and Matt Gaetz circulated it. *The Hollywood Reporter*, *TMZ*, *NBC News*, *CBS Sacramento*, and *CNN* all covered it within the same week.

The Pratt campaign did not make it. The Pratt campaign did not pay for it. The Pratt campaign did not approve it. A Los Angeles AI filmmaker named Charlie Curran — Menace Studios, four people on staff, formerly a commercial filmmaking veteran — made it on his own over what was probably a weekend, using a generative video tool called Seedance 2.0. Curran is not on the campaign payroll. He did not respond to NBC News when they asked him why he made it.

That sentence — *the most-watched political ad in the race was made by a supporter with no formal campaign relationship, using consumer-accessible AI tools, on a weekend, for free* — is the story. Spencer Pratt is not the story. Spencer Pratt is the unusually clean example through which we can see the story.

## Supporters are now the ad agency

For most of campaign-communication history, you needed a campaign to make campaign media. The production cost gate — the agencies, the equipment, the talent, the post-production, the broadcast time — kept the supplier population small and the content concentrated. Supporters could meme, retweet, hand out yard signs, knock doors. They could not, by themselves, produce ninety seconds of cinematic dystopian political video by Friday night.

That gate has collapsed. Not entirely — the cost of *knowing what to make* and the cost of *knowing what will resonate* are still real, and we'll get to the second one in a minute — but the cost of *making* a piece of cinematic political video has fallen by roughly an order of magnitude in the last two years. Anyone with a Seedance subscription and a decent narrative imagination can now produce, at home, the kind of imagery that in 2020 required a production company. In Curran's case, the imagination is unusually good and the production sensibility is professionally trained. But the point is that he didn't *need* a campaign to back him up. He needed an internet connection and a weekend.

This is what people miss when they cover Pratt as a celebrity stunt. The celebrity-stunt framing is wrong on its own terms — Pratt is polling at 22% in a credible Emerson poll, has out-raised the incumbent in the current cycle, is widely scored as having won a televised debate after which Karen Bass canceled the next one, and has the second-place position in a three-way race with a Democratic incumbent and a sitting councilwoman. But it's also wrong as media analysis. The story is not that a reality-TV personality is running for mayor. The story is that a reality-TV personality running for mayor has become the most cinematically ambitious campaign in Los Angeles without his campaign producing most of the cinematic content.

## The four artifacts

Four pieces of content tell you what's actually happening.

The **Batman ad** (Curran / Menace Studios, supporter-generated, early May 2026). 3.9 million views. Elite-amplified. Not commissioned. The case in chief.

The **"Spencer, Saca La Bassura" video** (Adrian Alvarez / Latinos Por Pratt, supporter-generated, March 30, 2026). A salsa music video puns on Bass's name and the Spanish word for trash. Pratt wheels out a trash bin containing the mayor. It has its own domain — sacalabassura.com.

The **Fresh Prince of Bel-Air parody** (Pratt's own Instagram, May 18, 2026). Pratt himself raps a version of the *Fresh Prince* theme about the Palisades fire displacing his family. This is campaign-produced — Pratt is the producer. He ends the video pulling up to the Hotel Bel-Air, which provokes immediate backlash and a KTLA story in which he has to deny living at a luxury hotel.

The **"They Not Like Us" video** (campaign-commissioned, April 2026). A play on Kendrick Lamar. Pratt films outside Bass's mansion, then outside Councilwoman Nithya Raman's "$3 million mansion," then pivots to his own Airstream on the scorched lot where his Palisades home stood. The campaign paid for this one.

If you only look at the artifacts, you'd see a coherent aesthetic — cinematic, mythic, dystopian, outsider, sometimes funny, sometimes dark. If you look at the *attribution*, you see something stranger: two of the four most-talked-about ads in the campaign weren't made by the campaign. The campaign made the rap parody itself and commissioned one cinematic piece. The most viral video in the race — the Batman ad — was made by a supporter the campaign has never paid.

This is the configuration. It is not a Pratt configuration. It is a 2026 configuration. It will be a 2028 configuration. We should be honest about what it changes.

## What it changes, in three sentences

**It partially offsets the money asymmetry between incumbents and challengers.** Karen Bass has $2.3 million in cash on hand. Bass has, as far as I can tell, no equivalent of the Batman ad — no piece of broadcast-quality political video that drove 3.9 million views and got Jeb Bush to call it the best political ad of the year. She has more money than the Pratt campaign has. She does not have more reach.

**It changes who needs to be on the campaign org chart.** If the most-watched ad of the race is made by a guy you've never met, the relevant campaign capability is not the in-house production team. It is the *curatorial* team — the people who watch supporter content as it appears and decide which pieces are good enough to repost. In 2028, every serious presidential campaign will need a supporter-relations function whose explicit job is to triage incoming supporter-generated AI content. The campaigns that don't will get out-produced by the campaigns that do. The campaigns that have one but don't have the cultural fluency to triage well will repost the wrong things.

**It creates a structural space of "attributable but deniable" campaign content.** When Bass went on CNN to call the AI videos "a very dangerous trend" — she specifically cited a clip showing her and Newsom drowning in a reservoir — she was raising a question for which the present infrastructure has no answer. Who is accountable for that clip? The supporter, who made it? Pratt, who reposted it? Both? Neither? Pratt's own description of the videos as "fan-made" — in a now-deleted Instagram clip — is precisely the move the configuration enables. He's not the author. The supporter isn't officially affiliated. The amplification is "just" a repost. And the candidate clearly benefits from the reach.

## What it doesn't change, in one sentence

It doesn't change the fact that *being a supporter doesn't automatically mean you understand the community you're producing for*.

Here is the most underrated story in the Pratt media ecosystem. Gustavo Arellano wrote a *Los Angeles Times* column in April arguing that "Spencer, Saca La Bassura" — the Latinos Por Pratt video — reads as Miami, not Los Angeles. The salsa instrumentation is wrong; the Cuban Spanish lexicon (*carajo*, *fájame*, *mi gente*) is wrong; the whole sensibility is wrong for a city whose Latino population is overwhelmingly Mexican. Adrian Alvarez, who runs the Latinos Por Pratt account, is Cuban American and splits his time between Miami and Los Angeles. Arellano's read, on the cultural evidence he marshals, is basically correct. A corrido in Mexican Spanish would have fit. A salsa in Cuban Spanish does not.

This is the failure mode that will define the next two election cycles. Generative AI lowers the cost of *producing* culturally targeted content. It does not lower the cost of *knowing which culturally targeted content will resonate*. A supporter who knows the community produces well. A supporter who only thinks they know the community produces poorly. In an open-source production environment, both kinds of supporter can produce — and both kinds will get reposted by candidates who don't have the cultural fluency to distinguish them.

This is also why I am skeptical of the strongest version of the "supporters are the new ad agency" thesis. The supporters are *a* new ad agency — one with extraordinary speed, extraordinary creative range, and very uneven quality. The campaigns that will benefit most from the configuration are the campaigns that develop the curatorial discipline to amplify the Curran-quality artifacts and quietly let the Alvarez-style misfires pass. The campaigns that just repost everything sympathetic will accumulate cultural-fit failures at scale, and the *Los Angeles Times* will write the column.

## What 2028 actually looks like

Here are six things I expect to be true about the 2028 cycle. None of them are forecasts. All of them are likely emergent patterns.

The number of supporter-generated AI artifacts per major campaign will move from a handful (as in 2026) to dozens or hundreds per week. The technology that allowed Curran to produce the Batman ad on a weekend in 2026 will be cheaper, more capable, and more widely distributed by 2028. The supplier population at the supporter tier will grow. The Curran tier — talented, professional, ideologically aligned, producing without payment — will get larger and more visible.

Campaigns will need a triage function. Not the digital team's afterthought; a dedicated capability, with staff, with a process, with cultural-fit subject-matter experts in the rooms where decisions to repost get made.

The political press will need a vocabulary for "amplification as authorship." When the candidate reposts the supporter, the press will need to be able to say what that is — neither pretending it's wholly the candidate's content nor pretending it's wholly the supporter's content. The vocabulary doesn't exist yet. It will.

The platforms will face renewed pressure to label, attribute, or otherwise mark this content. They may or may not respond. The regulatory bodies will face the same pressure. They probably won't respond in time.

Cultural-fit failures will become more visible. They will become a major source of the news cycles that hurt campaigns. The campaigns that build curatorial discipline will avoid them; the campaigns that don't will produce a steady drip of awkward "this ad doesn't actually speak to the community it's trying to reach" stories.

And finally — this is the one I am least sure about, and the one most worth thinking carefully about — we are going to learn whether supporter-generated AI media actually *persuades* anyone, or whether it mostly *consolidates* the sympathies of people already on the candidate's side. The Pratt case does not, by itself, tell us. He went from 10% in March to 22% in May, but the undecided pool also collapsed from 51% to 16%, and a lot of his gain probably came from undecideds breaking late rather than from any particular video moving any particular voter. The 2028 cycle will give us the data the 2026 cycle does not.

If it turns out that the configuration mostly consolidates rather than persuades, the strategic implications are very different from the implications we'd draw if it persuades. Consolidation is valuable but limited. Persuasion is transformative. The field — the academic field of political communication, the practitioner field of campaign strategy, and the regulatory field of election integrity — needs to know which one this is. We don't yet.

## So: Spencer Pratt

If the LA mayoral primary on June 2 goes the way the polling suggests, Karen Bass survives this. Pratt does well; he does not win; he becomes a 2026 footnote and a 2028 case study. If the polling is wrong and Pratt does better than 22%, the case study is louder, but the underlying configuration is the same one I've described.

The candidate is, as I said at the top, not the story. The configuration is the story. The next mayoral race, the next congressional race, the next presidential race will all be fought, in part, on the production layer Curran and Alvarez have opened. The campaigns that show up to that fight with the wrong organizational chart, the wrong curatorial discipline, and the wrong assumptions about what supporters can and cannot do for them will be at a meaningful disadvantage. The ones who show up well-prepared will, I think, win some races they would have lost in the prior configuration.

That is the takeaway from Los Angeles in the spring of 2026. The takeaway is not Spencer Pratt. The takeaway is the four people in a Los Angeles studio making the most-watched political ad in the city without anyone on the campaign asking them to.
