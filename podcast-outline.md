## Working titles (pick one)

1. **"Supporter as Studio: How Spencer Pratt's Mayoral Run Foreshadows 2028"** *(academic-leaning; best for political-communication or tech-policy podcasts)*
2. **"The Most-Watched Ad in LA Wasn't Made by a Campaign"** *(provocative; best for crossover and mainstream-political podcasts)*
3. **"Charlie Curran, Adrian Alvarez, and the New Political Ad Agency"** *(supplier-centered; best for tech / AI-focused podcasts)*

Use #1 for tone-matched academic audiences, #2 for crossover. Avoid #3 unless the audience knows or wants to know the supplier names.

---

## Episode-length target: 22–25 minutes

Long enough to develop the argument; short enough to listen to on a single commute.

---

## Cold open (90 seconds)

Open with the Batman ad — describe it, scene by scene. "Mayor Karen Bass in Joker makeup. Gavin Newsom eating cake like Marie Antoinette. Kamala Harris chugging vodka. Citizens dragged before them, ridiculed for asking for help with homelessness and wildfire recovery. Then a man in a Batman costume — Spencer Pratt, the reality TV personality — leads a tomato-throwing crowd to take the city back. The ad ran on X. 3.9 million views in about a day. Jeb Bush called it 'maybe the best political ad of the year.' Ted Cruz amplified it. Matt Gaetz amplified it. The Pratt mayoral campaign did not make it. The Pratt campaign did not pay for it. The Pratt campaign did not approve it. One Los Angeles AI filmmaker — Charlie Curran, four-person studio called Menace Studios — made it on a weekend, for free, with no relationship to the campaign at all. That sentence is the story. Spencer Pratt is the unusually clean example through which we can see it. Today we're going to talk about what that example tells us about where political communication is going, and what 2028 is going to look like."

---

## Segment 1 — The configuration, not the candidate (5 minutes)

**Main point:** the production-cost gate on cinematic political video has collapsed. The supplier population for political ads has expanded from professional vendors to AI-fluent supporters.

Beats to hit:

- What the production gate used to be (agencies, equipment, talent, post-production).
- What's changed in the last two years (consumer-accessible generative video tools — Seedance, Veo, etc.).
- What this enables (a one-person studio producing a 90-second cinematic short film over a weekend).
- The 2028 implication: this is not a Pratt thing, this is a 2028 thing.

**Reusable one-liner #1:** *"For most of campaign history, you needed a campaign to make campaign media. That gate has collapsed."*

---

## Segment 2 — The four artifacts (6 minutes)

**Main point:** walk through the four media pieces that map the typology. Use them to introduce the three-tier framework.

The four:

1. **Batman ad** — Curran / Menace Studios. Supporter-generated. The case in chief.
2. **"Spencer, Saca La Bassura"** — Alvarez / Latinos Por Pratt. Supporter-generated. The cultural-fit failure case.
3. **Fresh Prince of Bel-Air parody** — Pratt's own Instagram. Campaign-produced.
4. **"They Not Like Us"** — campaign-commissioned. Tier-blurry.

End by introducing the typology: Tier I (campaign-produced), Tier II (supporter-generated AI), Tier III (journalist/influencer amplification). Then the key analytic point — the tiers leak. Pratt reposts Tier II from his Tier I channels; Bush and Cruz are Tier III amplifying Tier II content. The configuration is a system, not a sequence.

**Reusable one-liner #2:** *"Two of the four most-talked-about ads in the LA mayoral race weren't made by any campaign."*

---

## Segment 3 — The cultural-fit failure mode (4 minutes)

**Main point:** the cost of production has fallen; the cost of cultural fluency has not. The "Saca La Bassura" case is the cleanest available illustration.

Beats:

- Describe the video and the demographic context — LA's Latino population is overwhelmingly Mexican.
- Describe Arellano's *LA Times* critique — salsa where a corrido would fit, Cuban Spanish in a Mexican city, Miami sensibility.
- Make the broader point: supporters can produce, but supporters who don't know the community produce poorly, and an open-source production environment doesn't filter that automatically.
- Practitioner implication: the curatorial function (deciding which supporter content to amplify) becomes a real campaign capability for 2028.

**Reusable one-liner #3:** *"Generative AI lowers the cost of producing culturally targeted content. It does not lower the cost of knowing which culturally targeted content will resonate."*

---

## Segment 4 — Attribution, accountability, and the "fan-made" frame (3 minutes)

**Main point:** the configuration creates a space of attributable-but-deniable campaign content, and the field doesn't yet have the tools to address it.

Beats:

- Bass on CNN: "very dangerous trend," specifically citing the reservoir-drowning clip.
- Pratt's "fan-made" framing — the attribution-evading move the configuration enables.
- The supporter is real, the content is not formally campaign content, and the candidate benefits from the reach. Who's accountable?
- The 2028 implication: platforms, courts, regulators, and the press will all need new vocabulary; one or more of them will probably get it wrong before they get it right.

---

## Segment 5 — What 2028 looks like (3 minutes)

**Main point:** six likely emergent patterns. Frame as "things I am fairly confident will be true" rather than predictions.

Quick run:

1. Tool capability will improve; supplier population will grow.
2. Triage capacity will become a serious campaign function.
3. The amplification stack will adapt to expect supporter content as normal input.
4. The persuasion-versus-consolidation question becomes the central empirical question for the field.
5. Cultural-fit failures will be more visible, will produce bad news cycles, will hurt campaigns.
6. Attribution and accountability questions will demand normative or regulatory response.

End the segment with the practitioner takeaway: *"Campaigns that treat the supporter tier as exogenous noise will be out-produced. Campaigns that treat it as a paid-media substitute will be out-priced. Campaigns that treat it as a curatorial problem will be best positioned."*

---

## Close (90 seconds)

Bring it back to the cold open. The story is not Spencer Pratt. The story is the four people in a Los Angeles studio making the most-watched political ad in the city without anyone on the campaign asking them to. That is what 2028 is going to look like — at scale, across every serious campaign, with all the upsides and all the downsides we've talked about today.

**Closing line:** *"Spencer Pratt is not the story. The configuration is the story. The campaigns that figure out the configuration are going to win some races they would have lost. The ones that don't are going to lose some races they should have won. That's what we should be watching for."*

## Production notes for a host-guest version

If recorded as host-and-guest rather than solo:

- The guest (the analyst) leads Segments 1, 3, and 5. The host can usefully drive Segment 2 (the artifact walkthrough) as the "naive observer" and Segment 4 (attribution) as the "what do we do about it?" question.
- Hold one-liner #1 for the host to introduce; hold one-liner #5 for the guest to deliver.
- Keep the cold open in the guest's voice. The Batman scene needs the analyst's specificity to land.
- Reserve 2–3 minutes at the end for unscripted Q&A. The persuasion-vs-consolidation question often produces the best back-and-forth and is worth leaving room for.
