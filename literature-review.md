The Pratt Study sits at the intersection of three literatures — hybrid media systems, supporter-generated political content, and the early empirical scholarship on generative AI in elections — and rests empirically on a substantial body of press reporting on the 2026 Los Angeles mayoral race. This page collects every source the project draws on, indicates how each one was used, and links to the original wherever possible.

Entries are grouped into four sections: theoretical and scholarly sources, primary press reporting on the Pratt campaign, primary press reporting on the post-primary phase, and convention and primary-source documents. Within each section, entries are listed roughly in the order they appear in the analytic argument rather than alphabetically.

<section data-lit-section="theory" data-lit-label="Theoretical &amp; scholarly">
<h2>Theoretical and scholarly sources</h2>

<div class="lit-entry">
<h3>The Hybrid Media System: Politics and Power</h3>
<p class="lit-citation">Chadwick, A. (2017). 2nd ed. Oxford University Press.</p>
<a class="lit-link" href="https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-hybrid-media-system-9780190696726">global.oup.com</a>
<p class="relevance"><strong>Master frame for the entire project.</strong> Chadwick&rsquo;s argument — that contemporary political communication operates through the interaction and mutual remediation of older and newer media logics, neither of which simply supersedes the other — is the theoretical scaffolding the journal article and conference paper both extend. The Pratt case is analyzed as a hybrid media system event, with the original contribution being an explicit production layer added to Chadwick&rsquo;s distribution- and amplification-oriented framework. Without this source the rest of the project does not cohere.</p>
</div>

<div class="lit-entry">
<h3>The Logic of Connective Action: Digital Media and the Personalization of Contentious Politics</h3>
<p class="lit-citation">Bennett, W. L., &amp; Segerberg, A. (2013). Cambridge University Press.</p>
<a class="lit-link" href="https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/logic-of-connective-action/">cambridge.org</a>
<p class="relevance"><strong>Foundation for the supporter-generated content account.</strong> Bennett and Segerberg&rsquo;s characterization of loose, identity-anchored, personalized political expression at scale is the closest theoretical predecessor to what supporter-generated AI content does. The conference paper uses connective action to explain why supporters produce political media without coordination, while extending the framework to address the production asymmetry generative AI introduces between connective actors with different production skills.</p>
</div>

<div class="lit-entry">
<h3>The Citizen Marketer: Promoting Political Opinion in the Social Media Age</h3>
<p class="lit-citation">Penney, J. (2017). Oxford University Press.</p>
<a class="lit-link" href="https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-citizen-marketer-9780190658052">global.oup.com</a>
<p class="relevance"><strong>The most important predecessor literature, and the one the original draft of the conference paper missed.</strong> Penney theorized supporters-as-media-producers before generative AI was a meaningful campaign technology. The revised conference paper engages Penney directly to clarify what is genuinely new about the Pratt configuration: not supporter production itself, which Penney already named, but supporter production at cinematic broadcast quality at near-zero cost. Engaging Penney is what allows the paper to make a careful novelty claim rather than an overstated one.</p>
</div>

<div class="lit-entry">
<h3>Convergence Culture: Where Old and New Media Collide</h3>
<p class="lit-citation">Jenkins, H. (2006). NYU Press.</p>
<a class="lit-link" href="https://nyupress.org/9780814742952/convergence-culture/">nyupress.org</a>
<p class="relevance"><strong>Foundational participatory-culture frame.</strong> Jenkins&rsquo;s account of participatory culture is the older root system from which both Penney&rsquo;s citizen-marketer work and the supporter-content analysis here grow. The revised conference paper cites Jenkins for the participatory-culture lineage and as a check against treating Pratt&rsquo;s parasocial audience as a unique phenomenon rather than a recognizable continuation of celebrity-anchored fan cultures.</p>
</div>

<div class="lit-entry">
<h3>Analytic Activism: Digital Listening and the New Political Strategy</h3>
<p class="lit-citation">Karpf, D. (2016). Oxford University Press.</p>
<a class="lit-link" href="https://global.oup.com/academic/product/analytic-activism-9780190266127">global.oup.com</a>
<p class="relevance"><strong>Context for campaigns as data-driven testing environments.</strong> Karpf&rsquo;s account is most useful for the post-Substack and practice-brief framings, where the question is how campaigns adapt their operations to a data-rich content environment. The Pratt campaign is not a clean Karpf case — it is, on the available evidence, less analytically disciplined than the campaigns Karpf describes — but the framework is the right baseline against which to read the curatorial-discipline argument in §4 of the conference paper.</p>
</div>

<div class="lit-entry">
<h3>Computational journalism and AI-mediated political information</h3>
<p class="lit-citation">Diakopoulos, N. (various). Northwestern University Computational Journalism Lab.</p>
<a class="lit-link" href="https://www.computationaljournalism.com/">computationaljournalism.com</a>
<p class="relevance"><strong>Methodological baseline for AI-mediated political content.</strong> Diakopoulos&rsquo;s work is the closest available scholarly research program on how AI is restructuring political-information production. His lab&rsquo;s outputs are cited as a referent for the tech-policy framing of supporter-generated AI media, especially in the attribution-and-accountability section of the conference paper.</p>
</div>

<div class="lit-entry">
<h3>Preparing for Generative AI in the 2024 Election: Recommendations and Best Practices Based on Academic Research</h3>
<p class="lit-citation">Persily, N., &amp; Tucker, J. A. (Eds.). (2024). Stanford Graduate School of Business / University of Chicago Harris School of Public Policy.</p>
<a class="lit-link" href="https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/publications/preparing-generative-ai-2024-election-recommendations-best-practices">gsb.stanford.edu</a>
<p class="relevance"><strong>The most current scholarly consensus on AI in campaigns at the time the project was drafted.</strong> Persily and Tucker&rsquo;s pre-election working group concluded that the most consequential AI-in-elections dynamics would emerge in adjacent and supporter spaces rather than in formal campaign communications — a conclusion the Pratt case supports empirically. The conference paper uses this source to justify why the supporter tier is the right object of analysis.</p>
</div>

<div class="lit-entry">
<h3>Political Machines: Understanding the Role of AI in the U.S. 2024 Elections and Beyond</h3>
<p class="lit-citation">Center for Media Engagement (2024). University of Texas at Austin.</p>
<a class="lit-link" href="https://mediaengagement.org/research/generative-ai-elections-and-beyond/">mediaengagement.org</a>
<p class="relevance"><strong>The fullest published synthesis of AI use in the 2024 cycle.</strong> The CME report&rsquo;s central finding — that the most consequential uses of AI in 2024 were production-cost compression rather than deepfake disinformation — is the empirical foundation for the production-layer argument the conference paper develops. The Pratt case is, on this reading, the 2026 cycle&rsquo;s clearest single illustration of the CME thesis.</p>
</div>

<div class="lit-entry">
<h3>Generative AI Generating Concerns: Citizens&rsquo; Perspectives During the 2024 European Elections</h3>
<p class="lit-citation">Farooq, A., van den Hoogen, E., Tulin, M., &amp; de Vreese, C. (2025). International Journal of Press/Politics.</p>
<a class="lit-link" href="https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/19401612251376060">journals.sagepub.com</a>
<p class="relevance"><strong>Audience-side check on the supporter-generated-content thesis.</strong> Farooq et al. find a persistent gap between European citizens&rsquo; perceived and actual exposure to AI election content — with obvious implications for any claim that audiences can reliably distinguish supporter-generated AI media from campaign-produced media. This source anchors the conference paper&rsquo;s argument that the &ldquo;fan-made&rdquo; attribution frame works because audiences are not well-positioned to verify it.</p>
</div>

<div class="lit-entry">
<h3>Generative Memesis: AI Mediates Political Memes in the 2024 USA Presidential Election</h3>
<p class="lit-citation">Lipschultz, J., &amp; Pavliva, B. (2024). arXiv preprint.</p>
<a class="lit-link" href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.00934">arxiv.org</a>
<p class="relevance"><strong>Empirical study of synthetic visuals in 2024.</strong> The &ldquo;Generative Memesis&rdquo; paper examined synthetic visuals and user engagement in the 2024 US presidential election and found that synthetic imagery often outperformed non-synthetic imagery on engagement metrics — but, the authors emphasized, not in ways that translated cleanly to persuasion. This is one of the few empirical sources directly relevant to the persuasion-versus-consolidation question the conference paper names.</p>
</div>
